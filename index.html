Disability Inclusion in the Workplace

Disability inclusion in the workplace in vital. To an individual, its important that you have the ability to be granted the opportunity to get your dream job no matter who you are. A disability should not discourageor hold an individual back from chasing their dream. Not only should they be granted the opportunity, but as they step foot into the office they should know that they are in a safe enviornment where their work will be needed and appreciated. To a company, disibility inclusion is not only the right thing, but it is factually proven to help the company in a significant way. Without disibility inclusion in the workplace, a company is simply not doing all it can to support their current employees. Companies that are not proactive to disability inclusion are missing out on qualified talent. Many employees who are disabled are often not looked at for job opportunites despite having the right qualifications. Despite wanting to work, people with disabilites are employed at a much lower rate than their peers. Only 33% of disabled Americans are employed while 77% of able-bodied employees are in the workforce. My mission is to change this. I wa t to bring awareness to the fact that many companies do not participate disability inclusion despite the significant benefits.

Disabling Bioethics

In our society there is a current tension between the disability movement and mainstream bioethics. Arguments are made that the field of bioethics decides who gets to live and who dies. This should be the opposite, when coming to bioethics there must be a “zone of trust” for disabled people. There is a recognition of the importance of developing key concepts and principles of remodeling people's principles on disabilities. There are also disadvantages of being in a disabled environment such as not being able to get a job because of having cerebral palsy. Able-bodied people administer prejudicial treatment toward those who are considered disabled. The main argument is that the difference between mainstream bioethics and the disability movement is the difference in experience when experiencing a disability. Sometimes people are even denied physician-assisted suicide and are forced to live in pain

This article starts by stating how in history, people with disabilities have always been overlooked and seen as “voiceless patients”. It then goes on to talk about the disability rights movement, and programs on campuses that are meant to bring disabled people together. There was a disconnect between people with vastly different disabilities and they were not aware that they were going through similar struggles. For people with disabilities, there are many places that are not easily accessible for people with very different disabilities, and it is hard for them to find ways to work their way around different spaces. With the help of the disability rights movement they were able to work toward eliminating the harmful biases surrounding public spaces and disabled people.

Disability inclusion in Education

Disability inclusion within education is a very important topic. The book Disability Inclusion and Inclusive Education by Sailaja Chennat addresses how persons with disabilities have been treated historically and how they're continued to be treated. Arranged disability studies try to examine the social, economic, and political factors that have caused the marginalization and oppression of people with disabilities. There is a stigma around children with intellectual disabilities that they cannot benefit from inclusive schooling. However this is incorrect, studies have shown that children with intellectual disabilities have adapted to the curriculum with the help of a support scribe and some additional time. By making these simple adaptations we can become more inclusive as a whole. Technology also has a big play in inclusiveness. While technology is improving by the second, this has opened many windows and opportunities for the disabled. New computer programs have been created to help comprehend material in education/workspace environments. Funding for this equipment is factored into taxes and federal funding. Overall we can all make a difference within disability in education, if everybody does their part, we can break the stigma.
